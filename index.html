Why This Newsletter?

AI is changing quickly, and none of us has to figure it out alone.

This newsletter is meant to create a shared space for community learning across Hobart and William Smith Colleges. It is a place to see what colleagues are trying, ask better questions together, share ideas and concerns, and learn from one another’s experiences.

Our goal is not to promote one “right” way to use AI. And we acknowledge that there is no such thing as an expert in AI. Instead, we hope to support thoughtful, respectful dialogue about how AI is shaping teaching, learning, work, creativity, and student experience. By sharing examples, resources, questions, and reflections, we can build a more informed campus conversation grounded in curiosity, care, and respect.

What We Learned from the Spring BoodleBox Pilot

This spring, Hobart and William Smith Colleges conducted a pilot of BoodleBox, a collaborative generative AI platform. Through surveys, workshops, office hours, classroom conversations, and a final wrap-up session, participants shared valuable insights not only about the platform itself, but about AI literacy, teaching, learning, and the role of generative AI at HWS.

While every participant had a unique experience, eight themes emerged consistently throughout the pilot.

8 Emergent Consensus Themes

Click individual themes to expand the full original verbatim text.

1
AI Literacy Is About Critical Thinking, Not Just Technology

Read Complete Paragraph →

A recurring message throughout the pilot was that using AI well requires judgment. Participants emphasized the importance of verifying information, checking sources, recognizing hallucinations, and understanding bias. Understanding how AI works matters but developing the judgment to know when to trust it, and when to question it, may matter even more.

2
AI Literacy Requires Conversation, Not Just Access

Read Complete Paragraph →

One of the clearest lessons from the pilot was that providing access to an AI tool is only part of the work. What seemed to have the greatest impact on participants' confidence and understanding were the conversations that happened around the tool: during class time, workshops, office hours, lunch discussions, and informal exchanges with colleagues or peers.

Participants valued opportunities to share ideas, compare experiences, troubleshoot challenges, and discuss concerns. The pilot reinforced that AI literacy is not something we build through access alone. It develops through reflection, dialogue, and community learning.

3
Students Have More Complex Feelings About AI Than We Sometimes Assume

Read Complete Paragraph →

One of the most interesting findings was that student concerns often extended far beyond classroom policies. Some students were excited about using BoodleBox in their classes, some were skeptical, and many were wrestling with questions about creativity, learning, fairness, equity, environmental impact, and the broader implications of generative AI in society. The pilot reinforced the need for spaces where students can engage in thoughtful academic conversations about these issues.

4
AI Works Best When It Has a Clear Purpose

Read Complete Paragraph →

Participants found BoodleBox most useful for specific tasks such as brainstorming, lesson planning, drafting, summarizing, creating presentations, generating images, studying, and adapting content for different audiences. The tool was less effective when users approached it without a clear goal. One of the strongest pieces of advice from participants was simple: start with a specific problem you are trying to solve.

5
Confidence Comes Through Experimentation

Read Complete Paragraph →

Many participants reported that their comfort with AI increased over the course of the pilot. The people who gained the most value were often those who spent time experimenting, comparing models, testing limitations, and reflecting on the results. Confidence didn't come from mastering every feature. It came from being willing to experiment, make mistakes, and learn along the way.

6
Equity and Access Matter

Read Complete Paragraph →

Participants repeatedly noted that institutionally supported AI tools can help create more equitable access for students. At the same time, the pilot highlighted significant differences in students' prior experiences, familiarity, and comfort levels with AI. Supporting AI literacy means addressing those differences intentionally.

7
Faculty Need Practical Examples and Resources

Read Complete Paragraph →

One of the most consistent requests was for clearer support materials. Participants asked for quick-start guides, classroom examples, tutorials, prompt examples, and resources that demonstrate how AI can be used in specific disciplines and contexts.

8
We Still Have Important Questions to Answer

Read Complete Paragraph →

Not every question was resolved during the pilot. Participants raised ongoing questions about privacy, security, environmental impact, bot ownership, source transparency, institutional policy, and the role AI should play in teaching and learning. Those questions remain important and will continue to shape future conversations and planning.

What Comes Next?

  • AI Literacy resources for students & faculty
  • Clearer support materials and tutorials
  • Practical classroom examples and use cases
  • Opportunities for conversation / student-center focus

"Perhaps the most important lesson from the pilot is that AI literacy is not about becoming an AI expert. It is about developing the knowledge, habits, and critical thinking skills..."

Global Guidelines

DEC AI Literacy Framework Spotlight

As AI continues to show up in our classrooms, workplaces, and everyday lives, it feels important that we have a shared way to talk about what it means to use these tools. The Digital Education Council (DEC) AI Literacy Framework offers a practical, multi-dimensional way to structure training and assessment.

This framework is not just about clicking buttons or running prompts. It challenges us to look deeper at data ethics, bias evaluation, critical judgment, and key human qualities—emotional intelligence, creativity, and domain wisdom—that grow even more valuable in an automated age.

🏖️ Week 1: AI Beach Read

Practice summarizing and filtering information on the fly.

Prompt

Copy this Prompt:

"Summarize this for a busy college instructor. Give me the five most important takeaways, three possible implications for students, and one question I should think about."

W2

🧴 Week 2: Syllabus Sunscreen

Review language with a focus on student clarity and accessibility.

Prompt

Copy this Prompt:

"Review this from the perspective of a student who is new to college. What might be confusing, intimidating, or unclear? Suggest revisions that make this warmer and easier to follow without lowering expectations."

Which suggestions felt right? Which ones missed your voice or your students?

W3

🛟 Week 3: Lazy River Brainstorm

Pick one activity, discussion, or assignment that feels stale.

Prompt

Copy this Prompt:

"Give me five ways to make this activity more engaging for college students. Include one low-prep option, one collaborative option, one reflective option, one creative option, and one option that does not require technology."

Which idea surprised you? Which one could you actually try this fall?

W4

🔥 Week 4: Campfire Reflection

Close out the month by using AI as an introspective thinking partner.

Prompt

Copy this Prompt:

"Ask me five reflective questions about how I want students to experience my course this fall. After I answer, help me identify one small change I could make."

What did you learn about your own goals? What's the one small change worth committing to?
